Profile N°03

Tadao Ando

Japan — b. 1941

Architect · Pritzker Prize Laureate

01 / Biography

Life & Work

Tadao Ando (b. 1941) is a self-taught Japanese architect from Osaka. Before turning to architecture, he worked as a boxer and traveled extensively through Japan, the United States, Europe, and Africa, studying buildings firsthand rather than through formal training.

Ando founded his studio, Tadao Ando Architect & Associates, in Osaka in 1969. He is best known for a restrained material palette of exposed, board-formed concrete combined with a masterful, almost spiritual use of natural light.

His architecture is closely associated with critical regionalism — buildings that respond to their site and Japanese spatial tradition while remaining resolutely modern and geometric in expression.

03 / Portfolio

Famous Projects

Ibaraki, Osaka — a concrete chapel where a cruciform slit in the wall floods the altar with daylight.

Chichu Art Museum

Naoshima, Japan — built largely underground so the art, light, and landscape take precedence over the building.

Rokko Housing

Kobe, Japan — terraced concrete apartments stepped into a steep hillside.

04 / Ethos

Design Philosophy

Ando designs with light as a building material in its own right, using raw concrete not as a cold surface but as a canvas for shadow and silence. His work asks visitors to slow down and notice how a space changes through the course of a day.
